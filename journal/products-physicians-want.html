Building Products That Physicians Actually Want

Efficacy gets you considered. Everything around it gets you adopted.

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

It is comforting to believe the best product wins on efficacy. Efficacy is what gets a product considered. What gets it adopted, and kept, is everything wrapped around the molecule: how it fits into the visit, how forgiving it is to technique, how it changes the economics of the practice, and how easily it can be explained to the person in the chair.

Physicians do not adopt data. They adopt confidence and fit. A product that is marginally better on a slide but harder to use, harder to explain, or harder to make money with will lose, quietly and repeatedly, to one that is easier on all three. The decision is rational. It is just rational about more than the endpoint.

Physicians do not adopt data. They adopt confidence, fit, and a story they can repeat.

Adoption is a daily calculation

Every time a provider chooses a product, they are running a fast, mostly unconscious calculation. Will the result be predictable enough to protect my reputation? Can I deliver it without adding risk or chair time? Can I defend it to a patient in one sentence? Does it help or hurt the economics of my day? A product that answers those questions well feels effortless to reach for, and effortless is what habits are made of.

This is why technique sensitivity is a commercial liability, not just a clinical footnote. A product whose results swing with the skill of the injector spreads inconsistent outcomes, and inconsistent outcomes erode the confidence that adoption is built on. Predictability, even at a slightly lower ceiling, often beats brilliance that cannot be reliably reproduced.

Design for the whole visit

The best products are designed with the provider’s entire day in mind, not just the moment of action. They produce a result the patient can understand and the provider can stand behind. They fit the workflow instead of fighting it. They come with a story simple enough to survive being retold a hundred times a week. And they make the economics of the practice work, because a product that is hard to profit from is a product that gets quietly deprioritized.

Understanding what physicians actually want, then, is not a marketing exercise layered on at the end. It requires sitting where they sit and feeling the friction they feel. That is a clinical insight and a commercial one at the same time, which is exactly why it so often falls through the gap between the two.

The product that wins the room is rarely the strongest on paper. It is the one that makes the provider’s day easier and their promise to the patient safer.

Build for the endpoint and you get a product that performs in a trial. Build for the visit and you get a product that performs in a practice. The market rewards the second one, because the market is not a study. It is ten thousand small, human decisions made under time pressure, and those decisions favor the product that feels like the easy, safe, repeatable choice.
