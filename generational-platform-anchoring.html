The Generation That Quit Drinking Is Coming for Aesthetics Next

Why Each Generation's Anchor Platform Decides How It Behaves, and What That Means for Who Walks Through Your Door

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

One of the more revealing shifts in consumer behavior over the past decade is that Gen Z drinks meaningfully less alcohol than the generations before it, a change that has unsettled an entire beverage industry built on the assumption that each cohort of young adults would drink roughly as much as the last, and the temptation is to explain it as a matter of health-consciousness or virtue, as though this generation were simply more disciplined. That explanation misses almost everything interesting about what is actually happening, because the decline in drinking is not primarily a story about willpower; it is a story about a generation shaped by fundamentally different formative conditions and, above all, by a fundamentally different anchor platform, one that rewards documentation, control, and authenticity over the performative excess that alcohol was long used to signal. Understanding why Gen Z stopped drinking turns out to be the key to understanding how it will approach everything else, including aesthetics, because the same forces that changed its relationship to alcohol are about to change its relationship to how it manages its own appearance, and the industry built for the previous generation is not ready for the one arriving next.

Every generation has an anchor platform, and it shapes the behavior

The organizing idea here is that each generation comes of age anchored to a dominant social platform, and that platform is not a neutral channel through which the generation communicates but an active force that shapes its values, its behaviors, and its sense of how life is supposed to be conducted. The generation anchored to Facebook, the generation anchored to Instagram, and the generation anchored to TikTok did not simply use different apps; they were formed by different logics, because a platform built around curated life updates produces one set of behaviors, a platform built around aspirational and aestheticized image produces another, and a platform built around raw, authentic, algorithmically surfaced short video produces a third, and the generation shaped by each carries that platform's logic into everything it does. The anchor platform is a primary driver of a generation's behavior precisely because it operates during the formative years, teaching an entire cohort what to value, what to perform, what to fear, and what a good life looks like, and the differences between generations that get attributed to vague shifts in values are frequently, underneath, differences in the platform that shaped them.

Gen Z's platform explains its sobriety

Apply this to the drinking decline and it stops being mysterious, because the generation anchored to a platform of constant documentation and algorithmically rewarded authenticity has a very different relationship to control and to performance than the generations before it. A cohort that grew up understanding that anything might be recorded, that its image is permanent and public and constantly surfaced, has strong reasons to prize control over losing it, and the drunken excess that prior generations used to signal fun and social belonging reads, to a generation that lives under permanent documentation, as a loss of control with lasting consequences rather than a harmless rite. At the same time the platform rewards authenticity over polish, which undercuts the performative dimension of drinking, the signaling of a good time, because the generation values the appearance of being genuinely well and in control over the appearance of partying, and it has wellness woven into its identity in a way that makes sobriety a status marker rather than a deprivation. The sobriety is downstream of the platform, a behavior shaped by a formative environment that prizes control, authenticity, and wellness, and it is a preview of how this generation will approach every decision about how it presents itself.

The next aesthetic patient is not the last one

This is where the analysis becomes urgent for aesthetics, because the industry has spent years building its model around the previous generation, the one anchored to a platform of aspirational, aestheticized image, whose relationship to appearance was performative and enhancement-oriented and comfortable with the visible pursuit of a more beautiful version of itself. The generation arriving now is different in ways that map directly onto its formative platform, approaching appearance through a lens of wellness rather than enhancement, prizing authenticity and control over polish and transformation, deeply skeptical of overt salesmanship, and forming its judgments through a platform that rewards the raw and the real over the aspirational and the staged. This generation is not going to want what the last one wanted, and it is not going to be reached the way the last one was reached, because it approaches aesthetics as an extension of wellness and self-optimization rather than as beautification, it prefers prevention and subtlety to correction and dramatic change, it distrusts the hard sell viscerally, and it judges outcomes and providers through a platform whose logic is authenticity rather than aspiration. The industry that keeps marketing enhancement to a generation that wants wellness, and polish to a generation that wants authenticity, and dramatic transformation to a generation that wants subtle prevention, will find itself speaking a language its next customers do not respond to.

Prevention, authenticity, and the platform-native pitch

The strategic implications follow directly from the diagnosis, because reaching the next generation requires understanding both what it values and the platform-native language it responds to, and both are different from what the industry is built around. This generation approaches aesthetics as prevention and maintenance rather than correction, which favors the earlier, subtler, wellness-framed intervention over the dramatic later one, and connects directly to the recognition that the category's future runs through a wellness and prevention framing rather than a beautification one. It prizes authenticity, which means it responds to the honest, the understated, and the genuinely natural result rather than the obvious and the performative, connecting to the recognition that the best work is undetectable and that the natural outcome is becoming the aspirational one. And it forms its judgments through a platform whose logic rewards the raw and the real, which means the industry must speak in that platform's native language rather than importing the aspirational, polished, salesy approach that worked on the previous generation's platform, connecting to the recognition that perception in this category is shaped by the platform far more than by the message. The generation that quit drinking because its platform taught it to value control, authenticity, and wellness will approach aesthetics through exactly the same lens, and the companies that understand this will build for prevention, authenticity, and platform-native honesty rather than for the enhancement, polish, and salesmanship of the generation before.

The Marga Perspective

The deepest point is that generational shifts in behavior are not vague changes in values but concrete consequences of the anchor platform that shaped each cohort during its formative years, and that a company which understands the platform understands the generation, while one that treats each new cohort as a slightly younger version of the last will consistently misread the people actually arriving. Gen Z's declining alcohol consumption is the clearest available preview of how the next wave of aesthetic patients will behave, a generation shaped by a platform of documentation, authenticity, and wellness that will approach its own appearance with a preference for control, prevention, subtlety, and honesty that the enhancement-oriented, aspiration-driven model of the previous generation does not serve. The industry that keeps building for the generation it already understands, marketing enhancement and polish and transformation to a generation that wants wellness and authenticity and prevention, is preparing to miss the largest demographic shift coming toward it, and the companies that will win the next two decades are the ones that understand that the generation which quit drinking is coming for aesthetics next, and that it will not want what the last one wanted.

Questions worth sitting with

Any company or practice serious about its future should sit with a few questions about the generation actually arriving. Are you building for the generation you already understand, or for the different one coming toward you, and do you know how its formative platform has shaped what it will want? Is your model built around enhancement and transformation, which the previous generation wanted, or around wellness, prevention, and authenticity, which the next one does? Are you reaching this generation in the platform-native language of authenticity that it responds to, or importing the aspirational, salesy approach that worked on a platform it does not live on? And the largest question: when the generation that prizes control, authenticity, and wellness arrives in your category, will your entire model of dramatic, performative, aggressively marketed enhancement be speaking a language it has already decided it distrusts?

Beyond the article

The recognition that each generation is shaped by its anchor platform, and that the next wave of aesthetic demand will behave in ways the current model does not serve, is one of the most important and least prepared-for shifts facing the industry, and helping companies and practices understand the generation actually arriving and build the prevention-oriented, authenticity-driven, platform-native approach it responds to is a central part of what I do. If your strategy is built around the generation you already understand and you have not seriously considered how the next one differs, that gap is worth closing before the demographic shift arrives rather than after, and it is a good place to begin a conversation.
