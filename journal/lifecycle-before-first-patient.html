Why Lifecycle Strategy Should Start Before Your First Patient Is Enrolled

The longest-lived products were planned as franchises before the first dose.

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

Lifecycle management sounds like something you do at the end, once a product is mature and the obvious growth is gone. The products that dominate a category for a decade were not managed into longevity. They were designed for it, as franchises, before a single patient was ever enrolled.

Enrollment is a fork in the road you pass only once. The population you study, the comparators you choose, the geographies you include, and the data you collect beyond the primary endpoint each quietly open or close doors you will desperately want years later.

By the time you need the data, it is too late to have collected it.

Optionality is designed, not discovered

A second indication, a combination therapy, a new formulation, a claim in a new population: none of these appear by luck. They are options you either build into the program early or forfeit forever. The marginal cost of collecting one more carefully chosen variable in a trial is small. The cost of not having it when a competitor forces your hand is an entire franchise.

This is the trap of the minimum viable trial. A program designed to do exactly one thing as cheaply as possible will get the product approved and leave it strategically naked. It has no second story to tell, no adjacent market to enter, no data to answer the question the market asks in year three.

Think in franchises, not filings

A franchise mindset changes the questions you ask at the very start. Not just, what do we need to get approved, but, where could this product go, and what would we need to have measured today to be allowed to go there tomorrow. The first study stops being an isolated event and becomes the opening move in a longer game.

It also changes how the organization behaves. Teams that see a franchise invest in the infrastructure a single product would never justify: the evidence base, the medical relationships, the brand architecture that later products can inherit. That shared foundation is why some companies launch their second and third products so much faster than everyone else.

A single product is a filing. A franchise is a strategy the first trial either enables or quietly forecloses.

Planning the lifecycle before enrollment is not over-engineering. It is refusing to let the first, narrowest version of a study accidentally define the widest ambition the product could ever have. Decide how far you might want to go before you take the step that fixes how far you can.
