The Business Model Behind Allergan’s Loyalty Engine

The loyalty program is not marketing. It is a financial product.

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

A great loyalty program in aesthetics looks like marketing and behaves like finance. Points, tiers, and rewards are the friendly surface, the part everyone sees. Underneath sits a system that quietly changes the economics of both the patient and the practice, and that system, not the promotional gloss, is where the real advantage lives.

Start with the patient. Accumulated points and status make leaving expensive in a way that has nothing to do with the product’s quality. A patient with a meaningful balance and a tier they have earned is not just choosing a treatment; they are protecting an asset. Switching means walking away from value they already own, and people are famously reluctant to abandon what feels like theirs.

A loyalty program is a switching cost you convince customers to build for you.

A switching cost you volunteer for

Now the practice. A well-designed program routes patient behavior toward the products tied to it, and it does so through the patients themselves, who arrive asking for the branded treatment because that is what earns them value. The practice is not being bribed so much as being pulled, by its own patients, toward the products inside the program. That is not a discount. It is a switching cost engineered on purpose and paid for by loyalty, not margin.

The elegance is that customers build the switching cost themselves, voluntarily, because from their side it feels like a benefit. Every point earned is a small brick in a wall that later makes leaving harder. The company has convinced its customers to construct their own lock-in and to enjoy doing it. Very few marketing mechanisms are that self-reinforcing.

Points are a currency, and currency is finance

Push the logic one step further and the financial nature becomes undeniable. A program that issues points patients hold and practices honor has, in effect, minted a currency. That currency has all the properties finance cares about: a float of value collected now and delivered later, breakage from points never redeemed, and redemption timing that can be shaped like any liability. The balance-sheet implications are real, and they are not a side effect. They are the point.

Seen through that lens, the program is less a promotion and more a financial network operating inside a healthcare company. It manages deferred value, engineers customer behavior, and creates switching costs, all while presenting to the world as a friendly rewards scheme. The marketing costume is not a disguise the company is embarrassed by. It is what makes a sophisticated financial instrument feel like a gift.

The genius is not the rewards. It is convincing customers to build their own switching costs and thank you for the chance.

Read this way, the best loyalty engines are among the most sophisticated financial products in healthcare, hiding in plain sight behind points and tiers. Anyone can copy the surface, the app, the rewards, the branding. What is hard to copy is the decade of accumulated balances and the switching costs that patients have already, willingly, built. That accumulated liability is, paradoxically, one of the strongest assets the company owns.
