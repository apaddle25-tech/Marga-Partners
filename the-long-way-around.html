The Long Way Around

Why I started Marga Partners — and why development and commercialization were never two disciplines.

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners
Founding essayThe Marga MethodCommercializationAesthetics industryMolecule to market

I never planned to become a pharmacist. I certainly never planned to begin my career working on one of the most iconic brands in medicine before pivoting into commercialization, loyalty strategy, consumer marketing, and lifecycle management. Looking back, none of it makes sense — until you zoom out.

Pharmacy is, I still believe, the most comprehensive clinical schooling in all of pharma. It trains you to read a clinical trial, interrogate the literature, and reason through complex pharmacology — subjects some other clinical specialties take a single class on. I loved the rigor. But I wanted to affect health at a more global level than a single prescription at a time, so I followed the science upstream, into the places where products are actually made.

From bench to brand

My career started in basic science and clinical pharmacology, running early- and late-stage development programs, including work on BOTOX Cosmetic®, a brand that helped define an entire category. Then I did something most scientists never do: I crossed the aisle into the commercial world these products live in. Marketing. Pricing. Loyalty. Medical education. Field strategy. Portfolio decisions. I worked across three of the industry’s largest companies, including its two biggest.

What that path gave me was not a title, but a vantage point. Because I had stood on both sides, I kept getting pulled into rooms that were, on paper, well outside my job description: loyalty strategy on Monday, a market research read on Tuesday, data architecture on Wednesday, Phase 3 design and pricing on Thursday, brand strategy on Friday — all in the same week. After enough weeks like that, you stop seeing a company as a set of departments and start seeing it as a single system.

Development and commercialization were never two disciplines. They are one continuous path — and almost nobody is designing it as one.

An industry hiding in plain sight

Somewhere in those years I fell for this industry, because aesthetics is stranger and more sophisticated than it looks. It runs on the rigor of pharmaceutical development, but it lives in a direct-to-consumer, out-of-pocket, non-reimbursable world that has nothing to do with prior authorizations and everything to do with how people want to feel. It behaves less like traditional pharma and more like the airlines, the hotels, and the luxury houses: brand, loyalty, and points economies wired straight into the company’s financials. FDA-regulated drugs and devices, sold like an experience.

Once you see it that way, you notice the same recurring problem across the industry. We have become experts in individual stages of the journey — clinical development, regulatory, marketing, sales — and too often fail to connect them into a coherent path. Great science reaches the market with the wrong story. Brilliant commercial ideas arrive too late to change the evidence. The seams between the stages are where value quietly leaks out.

Why Marga

In Sanskrit, mārga means the path: not just a road, but a disciplined way of navigating complexity toward a destination. That is exactly what the aesthetics industry has lacked, and exactly the way I have learned to work. Marga exists because too many organizations optimize individual decisions instead of designing the entire journey, from molecule to market to mature brand.

I started Marga Partners to raise the standard this industry holds itself to — and to have that standard set by someone who has grown up inside it, not studied it from the outside. I have spent my career moving through nearly every stage of this journey, from the science to the launch to the numbers behind it, and knowing the industry inside and out is exactly what lets me insist it can be held to a higher bar: not incremental decisions made in silos, but the rigor, coherence, and quantitative discipline it has too long done without.

This publication is where I will think out loud about that path, in public. Not just industry news, but interpretation. Not five trends, but the thing everyone is missing. And over time, our signature Marga framework — the Marga Method and the Marga Index — gives this industry the quantitative, structured language it has never quite had. If that is the kind of thinking you want in the room, that is what Marga is for.

Beyond the article

Marga exists to design the whole path as one — molecule to market to mature brand — instead of optimizing decisions in silos while value leaks at the seams. If you are carrying a decision that spans the distance between the science and the market, that is exactly the kind of conversation worth having.
