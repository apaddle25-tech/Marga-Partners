Injectables Behave More Like Airlines Than Pharmaceuticals

Yield management, loyalty tiers, and perishable inventory: sound familiar?

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

Injectables have far more in common with airlines than with the pharmaceutical industry that produces them, and once you notice the resemblance you cannot stop seeing it. Both sell a perishable experience, both obsess over yield, and both build their economics on loyalty rather than on the transaction in front of them.

Start with the inventory. An airline seat and a treatment appointment share the cruelest property in business: they expire the instant they go unused. An empty seat at takeoff and an empty chair at 3pm are both revenue that can never be recovered. Everything about how these businesses price and schedule flows from that single fact, and it is a fact traditional pharma, with its shelf-stable pills and reimbursed demand, simply does not face.

An injectable practice is a yield-management business that happens to involve a needle.

Perishable, priced, and loyal

Because the inventory is perishable, both businesses learn to manage yield. They price dynamically, fill capacity strategically, and think constantly about how to move demand to where the empty seats and empty chairs are. And because acquiring a customer is expensive, both lean hard on loyalty: status tiers, accumulated rewards, and a deliberate structure that makes the best customers feel recognized and reluctant to defect. The frequent flyer and the loyal injectable patient are, economically, the same person.

Contrast this with the model everyone assumes injectables should follow. Traditional pharma sells a reimbursed necessity, prescribed by a physician to a patient who often did not choose it and does not pay for it directly. The demand is clinical, the payer is a third party, and the relationship is mediated. Injectables invert all of that: a discretionary experience, chosen by the consumer, paid out of pocket, and repeated by choice. That is the airline’s world, not the pharmacy’s.

Managing the right business

The mistake, and it is a common and expensive one, is to run an injectable franchise with a pharmaceutical mindset: focus on the molecule, sell to the prescriber, treat each vial as a transaction. Companies that do this leave enormous value on the table, because they are ignoring the yield, the perishability, and the loyalty that actually drive the economics. They are flying a full aircraft while pricing every seat the same.

The companies that quietly win are the ones that manage injectables like the perishable, consumer, loyalty-driven business it truly is. They think about the lifetime value of a patient, not the margin on a syringe. They build loyalty structures that lock in frequency. They manage the practice’s calendar the way an airline manages its cabin. The needle is real, but the business around it is closer to travel than to tablets.

Run injectables like pharma and you sell vials. Run them like an airline and you own customers.

Injectables behave like airlines because, economically, they are airlines: perishable inventory, dynamic pricing, and loyalty as the real engine of profit. The sooner a company stops managing them like reimbursed medicine and starts managing them like a premium consumer service, the sooner it captures the value that has been sitting, unpriced, in every empty chair.
