The Economics Behind Every Syringe

Follow one syringe and you find the whole business model.

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

Pick up a single syringe and follow the money, and you will end up understanding the entire business. Its price has almost nothing to do with the material inside it. It is about everything that material carries with it: the brand, the training, the guarantee, the loyalty value, and the confidence a provider is able to charge a patient because of all of the above.

The cost of the actual goods is a small fraction of what the syringe sells for. The rest, the overwhelming majority, is the invisible system stacked behind it: years of clinical evidence, a field force that supports the account, marketing that made the patient walk in asking for it by name, and the switching costs that keep the practice ordering it instead of something cheaper. You are not paying for a milliliter. You are paying for the machine that put the milliliter in a trusted hand.

Nobody pays for what is in the syringe. They pay for everything that got it into their hand.

Price is a story, not a sum

This is why comparing two syringes on their contents, as if they were commodities, misses the entire point. Chemically similar products can command wildly different prices because price in this category is not the sum of the inputs. It is the value of the story, the trust, and the certainty attached to the name. The premium is not a markup on materials. It is the market’s price for confidence.

Consider what the provider is actually buying when they choose the more expensive option. They are buying predictability, so the result is defensible. They are buying a name the patient already trusts, so the conversation is easier. They are buying support if something goes wrong, and loyalty value that flows back to their practice. Every one of those is real, and none of them is in the syringe. They are in the system the syringe represents.

What the premium really prices

The uncomfortable implication for challengers is that matching the contents is the easy part. You can make a chemically excellent product and still be unable to charge what the leader charges, because you have not yet built the years of evidence, the trained providers, the brand the patient asks for, or the switching costs that justify the premium. The molecule is a commodity. The system around it is the asset, and the system is what the price is really measuring.

For incumbents, the same logic is a reminder of what they are actually defending. It is not the formulation, which will be copied. It is the accumulated trust and infrastructure that let a familiar syringe command a price a superior newcomer cannot. Protect the system, keep investing in the layers that justify the premium, and the price holds even when the contents no longer look special on paper.

The chemistry is a commodity. The confidence is the product. The price is measuring the confidence.

Follow one syringe all the way through and you find the whole business model laid out in miniature: a small amount of material wrapped in a large amount of trust, priced according to the trust rather than the material. Understand that, and you understand why, in aesthetics, brand and not chemistry decides where almost all of the margin goes.
