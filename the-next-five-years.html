The Next Five Years in Aesthetics, Including the Predictions Nobody Wants to Make

Nine forecasts for the category, several of which I expect to be unpopular

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners
PredictionsCategory strategyGLP-1sConsolidationTrust & accountability

Predictions are a form of accountability, which is exactly why so few people in aesthetics make specific ones, preferring the safety of trend language vague enough to be right no matter what happens, and I would rather be usefully wrong than uselessly hedged. So what follows is a set of concrete bets about where this industry is heading over the next five years, assembled from the same frameworks these essays have been building, and arranged roughly from the forecasts most people will nod along to toward the ones I expect to annoy a meaningful number of readers. The controversial ones are not included for provocation; they are included because I think they are true, and because the predictions worth making are the ones that cost something to be wrong about.

The word "regenerative" faces its reckoning. The borrowed legitimacy that the regenerative label has been trading on will not last the decade, because the gap between what the word promises and what the products deliver has grown wide enough to attract the attention of regulators, plaintiffs, and an increasingly sophisticated consumer, and at some point that gap becomes a liability rather than a marketing asset. The companies that built their positioning on the vaguest and most inflated version of the word will find themselves exposed when the scrutiny arrives, and the ones that described their biostimulatory mechanisms honestly will look, in retrospect, like the only adults in the room. The reckoning may come through a regulatory action, a wave of consumer backlash, or simply the slow erosion of a term that got used until it meant nothing, but it is coming.

Toxins commoditize, and the premium-molecule story stops working. The neurotoxin category is converging on a reality the industry has resisted admitting, which is that the molecules are, for most practical purposes, close enough to interchangeable that the differentiation has to come from somewhere other than the vial, and as newer entrants compress price and multiply choice, the premium-toxin narrative built on marginal duration or onset claims will weaken further. The winners will not be the products with the cleverest efficacy story; they will be the ones that built ecosystems around the molecule, the loyalty programs, the provider relationships, the accumulated trust that turns a commodity into a default, which is the same lesson the oldest products in the category have been teaching all along. Toxin is becoming a trust and distribution business wearing the costume of a science business.

Filler contracts before it recovers, and the recovery is led by technique, not by a new product. The filler category's trouble is not finished, and I expect further softening before any genuine turnaround, because the forces driving filler fear, the longevity revelations, the injector-supply glut, the algorithm that rewards the worst outcomes, do not resolve on the timeline of a marketing campaign. When the recovery does come, and it will, it will be led by a rise in the quality of outcomes rather than by any new molecule, because the category's problem was never a product deficiency and cannot be solved by another product. The companies waiting for a next-generation filler to save the category are waiting for the wrong thing.

The celebrity injector gives way to the trusted system. The era of the individual celebrity injector as the organizing unit of trust is peaking and will decline, replaced by trusted systems, brands, and standardized practices that offer consumers something the individual never could, which is reliability that does not depend on the reputation or the availability or the mood of a single person. This is the accountability gap resolving itself in the only direction it can, toward structures that consumers can trust without having to adjudicate every personality, and the practices and companies that build those systems will inherit the authority that the celebrity model cannot sustain.

GLP-1s do more to reshape aesthetics than any injectable launch of the decade. Here the predictions turn less comfortable. The most important force in aesthetic demand over the next five years will not originate in aesthetics at all; it will be the GLP-1 phenomenon, which is already altering faces at population scale through rapid volume loss and will redirect enormous demand toward volumization, skin quality, and body treatments, while simultaneously normalizing the idea of pharmacological self-optimization in a way that lowers the psychological barrier to aesthetic intervention broadly. The category should be planning around this as the defining demand shift of the period, and most of it is still treating GLP-1s as someone else's story.

The most valuable aesthetics company of the next decade may not sell a molecule. This one will irritate the manufacturers, and I mean it seriously: the durable moat in aesthetics is shifting from chemistry toward software, data, and practice intelligence, and it is entirely plausible that the most valuable company to emerge from this category over the next decade will be one that never sells an injectable at all, but instead owns the operating layer that practices run on, the intelligence that improves outcomes, and the data that everyone else needs. Molecules are increasingly commoditized; the systems around them are not, and value tends to migrate toward the part of a category that cannot be easily copied.

The private-equity roll-up thesis in aesthetics is partly broken, and it will show. The consolidation wave that treated aesthetic practices as a straightforward roll-up opportunity underestimated the one variable that actually determines outcomes in this field, which is the quality of the individual hand, and I expect a meaningful share of these roll-ups to struggle as they discover that industrializing a business built on individualized craft tends to industrialize its mediocrity along with its margins. The paint-by-numbers approach that scale rewards is the same approach that produces the outcomes patients have learned to fear, and a consolidation strategy that grows volume while degrading outcomes is building on a foundation that trust will eventually undermine. Not every roll-up will fail, but the ones that mistook aesthetics for a pure operational scale play will learn that this category punishes exactly that mistake.

A major safety or regulatory event forces the standardization the industry refused to build voluntarily. This is the prediction I least want to be right about. The widening gap between the number of people injecting and the depth of training behind them is a structural risk that compounds every year, and structural risks of this kind tend to resolve through a precipitating event rather than through foresight, which means that at some point a sufficiently visible safety failure or regulatory intervention will force the credentialing and standardization that the industry has had every opportunity and no incentive to build on its own. The standards are coming either way; the only question is whether they arrive by design or by disaster, and the industry's track record suggests disaster.

The category finally attempts its "Got Milk" moment, and probably underfunds it. As penetration growth becomes harder to achieve through share-stealing alone, the logic of category-level demand-building will become impossible to ignore, and I expect the first serious attempt at a coordinated, category-growing effort to emerge within the period. I also expect it to be underfunded and half-committed, because the collective-action problem that has prevented it for years does not disappear simply because the need becomes obvious, and the first attempt will likely serve as a flawed proof of concept rather than a triumph. The real category campaign, the one that works, probably comes later, built on the lessons of the first one's shortfall.

The Marga Perspective

Read these predictions together and a single through-line emerges, which is that nearly every meaningful shift coming to aesthetics over the next five years is, underneath its particular details, a story about trust and accountability finding their way back to the center of a category that spent years organizing itself around everything else. The regenerative reckoning, the toxin commoditization, the filler recovery, the fall of the celebrity injector, the software moat, the roll-up correction, the coming standardization, all of them describe a market slowly being disciplined by the reality that outcomes and trust are what actually compound, and that the strategies built on borrowed language, interchangeable molecules, industrialized mediocrity, and unaccountable influence are the ones with the shortest runway. The companies that will win the next five years are not the ones with the best guess about which molecule comes next; they are the ones that understood, earlier than their competitors, that this was always a trust business, and built accordingly.

Questions worth sitting with

Any leadership team looking at the next five years should be willing to sit with the questions these predictions raise.

  1. If the regenerative label faces a reckoning, which side of it is your positioning on?
  2. If your molecule is commoditizing, what are you building around it that cannot be copied?
  3. If GLP-1s are the defining demand shift of the period, where is that reflected in your strategy, and if it is not, why not?
  4. And the largest one: if trust and accountability are the forces quietly disciplining this entire category, are you building for them deliberately, or waiting to be corrected by them?
Beyond the article

Predictions are only useful to the leaders who act on them early, and helping companies read where the category is heading, and position for the shifts that matter most while their competitors are still reacting to the last ones, is a central part of what I do, particularly for teams making the multi-year bets, launches, portfolio moves, platform investments, whose payoff depends entirely on getting the direction of the category right. If you are placing a bet that has to survive the next five years, it is worth pressure-testing against where this industry is actually going rather than where it has been, and that is a good place to begin a conversation.
