What Aesthetics Can Learn From Peloton's Pandemic Hangover

Mistaking a Spike for a Slope

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners
DemandSpike vs trendForecastingOverexpansionGrowth

Refractions: what aesthetics can learn from industries that have nothing to do with it.

For a brief and intoxicating stretch during the pandemic, Peloton looked like one of the great businesses of the era, its connected exercise bikes selling as fast as it could make them to a population trapped at home with closed gyms, disposable income, and a sudden urgent interest in working out in the living room, and the company's valuation soared to reflect what everyone assumed was a permanent transformation in how people would exercise. The assumption was wrong. When gyms reopened and life resumed and the specific conditions that had driven the surge evaporated, demand fell hard, inventory piled up, the valuation collapsed, and the company spent the following years in a painful contraction, cutting staff and leadership and struggling to find the smaller, truer size of its actual market. The mistake at the heart of the story was not a product failure, because the product was fine and the company real; the mistake was interpretive, a boom driven by extraordinary and temporary conditions read as a new permanent baseline, and it is a mistake that aesthetics, fresh off its own pandemic surge, is in real danger of repeating.

The spike was made of conditions, not converts

The crucial thing to understand about Peloton's rise is that it was assembled from a rare and unrepeatable alignment of conditions, the closed gyms, the confinement, the stimulus-inflated disposable income, the sudden salience of home fitness, and none of those conditions were permanent, which means the demand they produced was always a spike rather than a slope. The company's error was to treat the peak of the wave as the height of a rising tide, to build its cost structure and its expectations around the assumption that the surge represented a durable shift in behavior rather than a temporary response to temporary circumstances, and when the circumstances reverted, so did the demand, leaving a business sized for a market that had never actually existed at that scale. The distinction between demand made of durable converts and demand made of temporary conditions is the entire lesson, because the two look identical on a revenue chart at the moment of the boom and could not be more different in what happens next.

Aesthetics ran the same experiment

Aesthetics should find this deeply familiar, because it lived through precisely the same dynamic, a pandemic surge assembled from the same unrepeatable conditions, the confinement, the video-call self-scrutiny, the stimulus-inflated disposable income, the sudden cultural permission, and the forecasts that were revised upward on the assumption that the surge represented a new and permanent trajectory. The filler market in particular rode that wave, and the industry adjusted its expectations to match a peak that was never sustainable, and the subsequent softening has been widely misread as a mysterious decline when a significant part of it is simply the Peloton correction, a market returning from an anomaly to its actual baseline as the temporary conditions that inflated it evaporated. Understanding that a portion of the aesthetic softening is a Peloton-style reversion rather than a structural collapse is essential to responding correctly, because a correction and a collapse call for entirely different actions, and mistaking one for the other leads either to complacency or to panic, both of which are expensive.

The correction is not the whole story either

The final and most important refinement is that Peloton also teaches the opposite caution, that a correction narrative can become too comfortable, because Peloton's troubles were not only a reversion but also revealed real weaknesses in the underlying business that the boom had concealed, and the same is true of aesthetics. A market returning from a spike is not automatically healthy at its baseline, and the honest reading of a post-boom contraction requires separating the portion that is simple reversion from the portion that reveals a deeper problem the surge was hiding, which in the case of filler is a genuine erosion of trust that the boom masked and the correction exposed. Peloton is therefore a double lesson, a warning against mistaking a spike for a slope on the way up and a warning against mistaking a structural problem for a mere correction on the way down, and aesthetics needs both halves, because its current softening is almost certainly some of each, and telling the two apart is the difference between spending against the right problem and the wrong one.

Beyond the article

Distinguishing a temporary correction from a structural decline is one of the most consequential and most frequently botched judgments a company in aesthetics can make, and helping leadership teams read a post-boom softening accurately, separating the reversion from the deeper problem it may be concealing, so they spend against the actual issue rather than a misdiagnosed one, is a central part of what I do. If you are staring at a softening number and cannot tell how much is reversion and how much is something worse, that distinction is worth getting right before you act on it, and it is a good place to begin a conversation.
