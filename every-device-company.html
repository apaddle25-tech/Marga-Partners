Every Device Company Eventually Wants to Be an Injectable Company

The Gravitational Pull of Recurring Biology Over One-Time Capital

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

There is a pattern in aesthetics that repeats so reliably it has become almost a law of the category: a company builds a successful energy-based device, a laser or a radiofrequency platform or an ultrasound system, establishes itself, generates real revenue, and then, sooner or later, begins looking longingly across the aisle at the injectable business, adding consumables, acquiring a biostimulator, chasing a topical, angling toward anything that behaves less like the capital equipment it sells and more like the recurring biology it envies. The industry tends to read each of these moves as an individual strategic choice, a particular company deciding to diversify, when what is actually happening is the same force acting on every device company in turn, a gravitational pull exerted by the structural superiority of the injectable business model over the capital-equipment one, and understanding that pull explains not only why device companies keep making the same move but why so many of them struggle to complete it.

Two business models wearing the same white coat

On the surface, device companies and injectable companies look like siblings, both selling into aesthetic practices, both promising better outcomes, both operating under the same regulatory sky, and this surface similarity obscures the fact that they are, underneath, almost opposite businesses. The device company sells a large, expensive machine to a practice, a lumpy and infrequent transaction that happens once every several years, after which that practice is essentially satisfied and the company must go find the next buyer, competing constantly to sell the next box into a market that saturates, riding a sales cycle that is long, capital-intensive, and driven by the practice's willingness to make a major equipment investment. The injectable company sells something entirely different, a consumable that is used up and repurchased continuously, generating recurring revenue from the same accounts without having to resell the machine each time, pulled through the market by patients who ask for the product by name, defended by a brand and a body of trust that compounds with every predictable result, and margined in a way that capital equipment cannot approach. One business hunts for the next transaction; the other collects a recurring one. One is pushed into practices by a sales force; the other is pulled through them by patient demand. The two could hardly be less alike beneath their shared costume, and every meaningful advantage sits on the injectable side.

Consumable Gravity

Name the force and its logic becomes clear: what pulls device companies toward injectables is consumable gravity, the structural pull of recurring, high-margin, patient-demanded biology on any business currently dependent on one-time, capital-intensive, practice-pushed equipment. The gravity operates on every device company because the injectable model solves precisely the problems the device model cannot escape, replacing the endless hunt for the next capital sale with a stream of recurring consumable revenue, replacing the long and cyclical sales cycle with continuous repurchase, replacing the practice-push dynamic with patient-pull demand, and replacing a saturating installed base with a franchise that deepens over time. A device company feeling this pull is not being fickle; it is responding rationally to the fact that the business it wishes it had is structurally sounder than the business it built, and the wish recurs across the category because the underlying asymmetry is real and permanent. The market prices this asymmetry openly, assigning recurring consumable biology the kind of multiple that lumpy capital equipment rarely commands, which means the pull is not only operational but financial, a constant reminder to every device company of the more valuable shape its revenue could take.

Why the move so often fails

The pull is universal; the successful completion of it is not, and the reason so many device companies reach for injectables and come away disappointed is that they underestimate how thoroughly the injectable business demands a different organization than the one they built. A company assembled to sell capital equipment develops a specific set of muscles, a sales force trained to close large transactions with practice owners, a commercial rhythm built around the equipment purchase, an organizational DNA oriented toward the machine, and none of those muscles are the ones the injectable business actually requires, which are the muscles of consumer brand-building, of generating patient-pull demand, of cultivating the trust that turns a consumable into a default and defends it against commoditization. Moving from capital equipment to injectables is not a product extension; it is a change of fundamental business model, from a B2B capital-sales operation to something much closer to a consumer brand, and a company that attempts the leap while retaining its capital-sales instincts tends to acquire the injectable asset and then manage it with the wrong reflexes, pushing it through a sales force when it needed to be pulled through by patients, competing on specifications when it needed to be building trust. The gravity is real, but so is the escape velocity required to actually reach the other side, and many device companies discover too late that wanting the injectable business is far easier than becoming it.

The deeper pattern

Consumable gravity is a specific instance of a more general truth about where value migrates in aesthetics, which is that it migrates toward whatever is recurring, defensible, and pulled through the market by the end consumer rather than pushed into it through the practice. Injectables are the clearest current expression of that, but the same gravity is what makes software, data, and practice intelligence increasingly attractive, because they too promise recurring revenue and a defensible position, and the device company that understands the real force acting on it will recognize that its problem is not specifically the absence of an injectable but the absence of any recurring, patient-pulled, defensible revenue stream at all. The strategic question is therefore larger than whether to add a consumable; it is whether the business can move from the wrong side of consumable gravity to the right one, and whether it has the organizational capacity to become the kind of company that side requires rather than merely the kind that wishes it were there.

The Marga Perspective

The aesthetics market rewards recurring biology and consumer pull and quietly penalizes one-time capital and practice push, and the single most clarifying question a company in this category can ask is which side of that divide its revenue actually sits on, because the answer determines its margins, its multiple, and its durability far more than the cleverness of any individual product. Device companies feel consumable gravity because the pull is real and the asymmetry is permanent, and the ones that will successfully answer it are not the ones that simply buy an injectable and bolt it on, but the ones willing to become a genuinely different kind of company, with the consumer-brand muscles and the trust-building discipline that the recurring side of the business demands. Wanting to be an injectable company is the easy part, felt by nearly everyone; actually becoming one is a transformation, and the market pays for the transformation, not the wish.

Questions worth sitting with

Any device company feeling the pull, or any investor evaluating one, should sit with a few questions before mistaking the wish for a strategy. Which side of consumable gravity does your revenue actually sit on, recurring and patient-pulled or one-time and practice-pushed, and what is that costing you in margin and multiple? If you acquire or build a consumable, do you have the consumer-brand and trust-building muscles to run it, or only the capital-sales muscles that will manage it badly? Is your real problem the absence of an injectable specifically, or the absence of any recurring, defensible, consumer-pulled revenue at all? And the question that separates the wish from the achievement: are you prepared to become a fundamentally different kind of company, or only to buy one and hope its DNA rubs off?

Beyond the article

The pull from capital equipment toward recurring biology is one of the most reliable and least understood forces in aesthetic strategy, and helping device companies decide whether and how to answer it, building the consumer-brand and recurring-revenue capabilities the move actually requires rather than simply acquiring an asset and managing it with the wrong instincts, is a meaningful part of what I do. If you are feeling consumable gravity and trying to work out whether your organization can actually reach the other side of it, that is a question worth answering clearly before the acquisition rather than after, and it is a good place to begin a conversation.
