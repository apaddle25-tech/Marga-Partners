The Marga Portfolio Model

How the products you own should make each other stronger.

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

The Marga Portfolio Model treats a portfolio as a system of relationships rather than a shelf of products. Its central question is deceptively simple and rarely asked: not what do we own, but how does each thing we own make the others more valuable? A portfolio that cannot answer that question is not a portfolio. It is a collection with a shared logo.

The model maps every product by the role it plays in the customer relationship rather than by its line on the revenue report. Some products open the relationship, the accessible entry point that brings a customer in. Some deepen it, growing the value of a customer you already have. Some defend the franchise against competitors. Some expand into adjacent needs. Roles, not revenue, are the organizing unit, because roles are how products actually relate to one another.

A portfolio is a system of roles. When two products play the same one, you are competing with yourself.

Roles, not just revenue

Ranking a portfolio by revenue tells you which products are big. Mapping it by role tells you whether the products form a system, and the difference is where strategy lives. Two products that generate healthy revenue but play the same role are not two strengths. They are one role, redundantly filled, quietly cannibalizing each other for attention and budget while the revenue report congratulates you on both.

The map makes those pathologies visible. Overlap shows up as two products crowded into the same role, a sign you are competing with yourself. Gaps show up as roles no product fills, no accessible entry point, or nothing defending the core, and every empty role is a door left open for a competitor to walk through. Neither the redundancy nor the gap is visible on a revenue list; both are obvious on a role map.

From collection to system

Seeing the portfolio as a system changes how you make decisions about it. Adding a product stops being a question of will this sell and becomes will this strengthen the system, does it fill an empty role or crowd a full one. Acquisitions get evaluated by fit, not just by the top line they add, which is why so many top-line-accretive acquisitions quietly subtract focus: they bought revenue and duplicated a role. The model turns a list into a strategy by insisting that every piece justify itself relationally, not just financially.

Managed this way, a portfolio stops being something heavy you carry and starts being something that carries you. The entry product feeds the deepening product; the deepening product funds the defense; the defense protects the whole. Each piece makes the next one easier to sell, and the portfolio compounds into a moat that no single product, however strong, could ever be on its own.

A portfolio is not what you own. It is the system your holdings form, and whether that system defends itself.

The Marga Portfolio Model is a discipline for refusing to mistake breadth for strength. It asks the only question that separates a portfolio from a pile: does each thing we own make the others stronger? Answer it honestly, map the roles, close the gaps, and resolve the overlaps, and a collection of products becomes a system, which is the only form a portfolio can take that a competitor cannot easily pull apart.
