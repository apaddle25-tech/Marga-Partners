What Aesthetics Can Learn From Vibe Coding

When a Confident Tool Removes the Expert but Keeps the Output, and Why the Judgment Was the Whole Job

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners
AutomationJudgmentExpertiseTechniqueDe-skilling

Refractions: what aesthetics can learn from industries that have nothing to do with it.

In early 2025 a term escaped from the software world into the general culture and named something that had been quietly happening everywhere, which was that people had begun building working software without knowing how to program, simply by describing what they wanted to an artificial intelligence and accepting whatever it produced. The practice was called vibe coding, and its defining move was to stop reading the code at all, to give in to the vibes, to let the machine generate and to trust that it worked, and for a certain class of simple projects it did work, well enough to feel like genuine magic and to convince a great many people that expertise had suddenly become optional. Then came the other half of the story, the moment the confidently-generated software broke in some way the person who had summoned it could not understand, could not diagnose, and could not fix, because the knowledge being bypassed was exactly the knowledge that would have caught the problem, and the tool that had removed the expert had kept the output while quietly discarding the judgment that made the output safe. Aesthetics, which lived through its own version of this story before software ever did, has a great deal to learn from what vibe coding reveals about what happens when a confident tool lets people skip the expertise that was never actually optional.

The tool democratizes the action, not the judgment

The lesson hiding inside vibe coding is a general one, and it applies far beyond software, because what these tools actually democratize is the action, the production of an output, while leaving entirely undemocratized the judgment required to know whether the output is any good and what to do when it fails. A person can now generate code, or a legal document, or a medical-sounding plan, or a skincare regimen, without any of the underlying knowledge, and the thing they produce will often look entirely credible, fluent and confident and plausible, which is precisely the trap, because the fluency of the output reads as competence to someone who lacks the expertise to see where it is wrong. The judgment was never the slow and annoying part of the work that could finally be automated away; the judgment was the work, the accumulated understanding that lets a professional look at a plausible output and know that it is subtly and dangerously incorrect, and a tool that produces the output while skipping the judgment has not made the expertise unnecessary but has merely hidden its absence until the moment it catastrophically matters. The confidence of the machine's output is, if anything, inversely related to the user's ability to evaluate it, which is what makes the whole arrangement so seductive and so dangerous at once.

Aesthetics already ran this experiment

Aesthetics should recognize all of this with a particular chill, because it has already run a version of the experiment at genuine scale, in the enormous consumer market for at-home devices, where the professional tool was miniaturized, wrapped in a friendly interface, and placed directly in the hands of people with none of the training that governs its use in a clinic. The at-home lasers and intense pulsed light devices, the microneedling rollers, the radiofrequency and microcurrent units, the peels sold for the bathroom counter, all of it follows the identical logic of vibe coding, democratizing the action, the treatment itself, while leaving entirely undemocratized the judgment that makes the treatment safe, the assessment of whether a given skin should be treated at all, the correct setting for it, the contraindication that rules it out, and the knowledge of when to stop. The devices are engineered to feel effortless and safe, a button and an app and a reassuring light, and that very simplicity reads as competence to a user who has no way to know that their skin is wrong for the device, or that the setting is too high, or that the condition they are treating is one that heat and light will quietly make worse. And the outcome follows the same arc as the code that runs beautifully until it does not, fine and fine and fine until the burn, the scar, or the stubborn hyperpigmentation that lands disproportionately on darker skin, or the melasma that the device was meant to fade and instead deepened, because the judgment being skipped was precisely the judgment that would have seen it coming. The industry knows, from a market it helped build, what happens when a confident tool removes the expert but keeps the treatment, and that hard-won knowledge is exactly what lets it see the next wave more clearly than most.

The second, larger wave is consumer AI

What is coming next is a second do-it-yourself beauty era, larger than the first, driven not by a device placed in the consumer's hands but by consumer artificial intelligence, and it is arriving now. The tools that analyze a face from a photograph and return a plan, the chatbots that produce a confident skincare regimen on request, the applications that rate a face and recommend the interventions that would raise the score, the simulations that show a person a transformed version of themselves, are all doing to aesthetic judgment exactly what vibe coding did to programming, handing people a fluent and authoritative-seeming aesthetic plan without any of the clinical judgment required to know whether it is right or safe for them. This is vibe aesthetics, the making of decisions about one's own face on the confident vibes of a machine that does not actually understand that face, its history, its skin, its risks, or the interactions and contraindications that a professional would catch, and the danger is identical to the one vibe coding exposed, a plausible and confident recommendation that is right until it is disastrously wrong. It connects directly to the ranking culture now driving so much demand, because the same young person being told by an application that his face scores poorly is being handed, by another application, a confident plan to fix it, neither of which carries the judgment to know whether any of it is true or wise, and the confidence of the output is exactly what makes it so hard to resist and so dangerous to follow.

The judgment is the defensible thing, and the duty

The strategic conclusion for the industry follows directly, and it has two halves that turn out to be the same half, because the judgment that consumer AI cannot supply is simultaneously the industry's most defensible source of value and its clearest professional responsibility. On the value side, a field whose practitioners try to compete with a free and confident machine on convenience or on the production of plans has misunderstood the game entirely, because the machine will always be faster and cheaper at producing the plausible output, and the only durable ground is the judgment the machine lacks, the assessment and the interpretation and the accountability that no confident generation can replace, which means the industry should be foregrounding and investing in judgment rather than racing the tool to the bottom. On the duty side, the profession's role in an age of vibe aesthetics is precisely to be the judgment layer that consumer AI cannot provide, the expert who catches what the confident tool got wrong, who tells the patient that the plan the machine generated is not safe for his skin or appropriate for his anatomy or wise for his psychology, and a field that instead builds vibe aesthetics into its own practice, leaning on the tools without supplying the judgment, abdicates the one thing that made it necessary. The judgment being bypassed is, in both senses, the whole job, and the industry that understands this will build its value and discharge its duty in the same motion.

The Marga Perspective

Vibe coding is a preview, a clean early demonstration of what happens across every field when a confident tool learns to produce the expert's output while skipping the expert's judgment, and aesthetics is better positioned than most to understand it because it already paid the price of this exact pattern once, in an era of do-it-yourself beauty whose consequences it has spent years absorbing. The companies and practices that navigate the coming wave of consumer aesthetic AI well will be the ones that recognize that the judgment the machine cannot supply is both their most durable advantage and their central responsibility, and that the correct response to a tool that democratizes plausible aesthetic plans is not to compete with it on plausibility but to become, unmistakably, the source of the judgment it lacks. The confident output is seductive precisely to the people least able to evaluate it, which is what makes vibe aesthetics dangerous and what makes genuine professional judgment valuable, and the field that foregrounds that judgment rather than hiding behind the tool will be the one that earns trust while the confident machines quietly generate their catastrophes.

Beyond the article

The arrival of consumer artificial intelligence as a source of confident, judgment-free aesthetic advice is one of the most important shifts the industry is about to navigate, and helping companies and practices position their genuine clinical judgment as both their durable competitive advantage and their professional responsibility, rather than racing the machines to produce plausible plans, is a meaningful part of what I do. If consumer AI is beginning to shape what your patients believe and want before they ever reach you, the strategic question of how you become the judgment those tools lack is worth working through deliberately, and it is a good place to begin a conversation.
