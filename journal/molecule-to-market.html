The Long Road from Molecule to Market

The value leaks out at the seams between stages, not inside them.

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

A product travels an enormous distance from molecule to market: discovery, development, regulatory, manufacturing, medical, marketing, sales, and finally the slow work of adoption. Each stage has its own experts, its own vocabulary, and its own definition of success. Individually, most companies run these stages well. The trouble is almost never inside a stage. It is at the handoffs.

Watch a product move down that line and you can see value evaporate at each transfer. The science says one thing, the label permits another, the marketing claims a third, and the field, standing in front of a customer, improvises a fourth. Every translation loses a little fidelity, and by the time the message reaches the market it can barely be traced back to the data that created it.

You do not need more specialists. You need someone who can see the whole road at once.

The seams are where strategy lives

Whoever can see the entire road can do the one thing no single specialist can: make the handoffs deliberate. They can ensure the endpoint the trial measures is the claim the label allows, which is the message the brand leads with, which is the sentence the field actually says out loud. When those four align, the product arrives in the market as one coherent argument instead of three lossy translations of an original nobody remembers.

This is not a communication problem to be solved with more meetings. It is a structural one. Organizations are built as vertical stacks of expertise, and nobody is rewarded for owning the space between two departments. So the seams go unmanaged, and the value that leaks through them is invisible on every individual scorecard.

Seeing the whole system is a skill

The people who can hold the entire path in their head are rare, and they are rare for a reason: the training in this industry runs deep and narrow. You become an expert in development, or in commercial, or in medical, and the incentives keep you there. Crossing those lines enough times to see the whole system is unusual, and it is exactly the vantage point the seams require.

It is also learnable, but only by doing. You earn it by standing at each junction, watching what gets lost, and refusing to accept that a scientific story and a commercial one must be different documents. Done well, the same idea survives from the bench to the bedside to the brand, gaining force at each step instead of leaking it.

A product is only as strong as its weakest handoff. Designing the handoffs is the strategy.

The long road from molecule to market is not a series of departments passing a baton. It is a single continuous argument that either holds together or falls apart at the joins. The companies that win are not the ones with the best specialists. They are the ones who made sure the specialists were building the same thing.
