The Wrong Endpoint

Why Neurotoxins Don't Wear Off When Their Trials Say They Do

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners
NeurotoxinsDuration of effectTrial endpointsPatient perceptionClaims strategy

There is a quiet, recurring disagreement in nearly every aesthetic practice in the country, one that plays out between the patient who is certain her neurotoxin wore off weeks before it should have and the injector who, looking at the very same face, is fairly certain it did no such thing, and the reason this argument never quite resolves is that both people are right at the same time, because they are measuring two different things and calling them by the same word. The patient is watching for the return of movement. The trial that brought the product to market was watching for the return of lines. And the several weeks that can separate those two events is where a surprising amount of the industry's confusion about how long its toxins actually last has quietly taken up residence.

To understand how a product can perform exactly as it was designed to and still feel, to the person wearing it, like a letdown, it helps to look at what the pivotal trials were built to measure in the first place, which was almost always the severity of a dynamic line, graded by a trained investigator on a validated photographic scale while the patient contracted the treated muscle as hard as she could, with the treatment counted as durable for as long as that line stayed meaningfully softer than it had been at the start. This is a rigorous and reproducible way to run a study, and it is also a way of looking at a face that no patient has ever adopted in her own bathroom mirror, because a patient does not frown at maximum effort and assess the residual depth of a glabellar crease against a four-point reference photograph; she notices, one ordinary morning, that her brow moved in a way it had not moved in months, and she reads that first flicker of motion, correctly, as the beginning of the end.

The difficulty is that motion and line depth do not come back on the same schedule. As a neurotoxin's effect wanes, motor function at the treated muscle recovers gradually, and a patient can regain enough movement to see it and feel it well before that movement has been going on long enough, or forcefully enough, to re-etch the dynamic line to the depth it began at. For a stretch of weeks, then, the product is doing precisely what its label promises on the scale the label used, holding the line softer than baseline, while the person it was injected into has already concluded, on the only scale she has ever cared about, that it is finished. Nothing has gone wrong with the molecule. It is simply being graded, out in the world, on an exam it was never designed to sit.

The result has two axes, and the trial scores only one

There is a further wrinkle in this, and it cuts deeper than a scheduling mismatch, because a neurotoxin result is not a single quantity that a patient wants more of; it is a balance between two things that pull against each other, the effacement of lines on one side and the preservation of natural movement on the other, and the modern patient increasingly wants both at once. The registrational scale, by its construction, rewards only the first of these, treating a face with less visible line and less visible motion as unambiguously the better outcome, which quietly encodes an aesthetic preference, the frozen look, that a large and growing share of patients now actively reject. So the trial endpoint is not merely out of step with how patients perceive duration; it is out of step with what patients have come to consider a good result at all, because the injector delivering the softly mobile, expression-preserving outcome that today's consumer prizes is, on the very scale that defines product efficacy, delivering a lower score. The market moved toward nuance while the measurement stayed anchored to a maximum, and the distance between those two positions is now wide enough to distort how the entire category understands its own products.

The wrong-endpoint problem

Once you see the gap between the trial endpoint and the lived endpoint, you start to notice that it is only the most visible instance of a much larger pattern, because a neurotoxin is held to a different standard by every party with a stake in it, and almost none of those standards agree. The regulator and the manufacturer grade the wrinkle scale, because that is what earns and defends an approval. The patient grades movement, and increasingly grades it through the unforgiving proximity of a front-facing camera. The injector grades whether the patient is happy enough to rebook and to refer, which relates to the wrinkle scale only loosely and to the movement question quite directly. And the investor grades revenue and the duration claims that get deployed in the competitive fight for it. The product sits at the center of all this, measured at once by four instruments calibrated to four different questions, after which the industry expresses genuine surprise when the answers come back in conflict. I have come to think of this as the wrong-endpoint problem, and it is worth naming precisely because it is so easily mistaken for something else, namely a product that underperforms, when what is actually happening is a product being asked to prove itself on a metric no one buying it has ever used.

Why this quietly costs the industry so much

The commercial consequences of this run larger than they first appear. A duration claim built honestly on the trial endpoint is, at the same moment, technically accurate and experientially misleading, and a claim that is true in the study and false in the mirror is one of the most reliable ways to erode a patient's confidence in a product that was, by its own design criteria, working the whole time. This is a self-inflicted wound the category keeps reopening, and it leaves an opening almost no one has moved to occupy, because the company willing to measure the endpoint patients actually experience, to study the return of movement and not only the return of lines, and to train injectors and educate patients around that lived timeline, would be the first to close the gap between what it promises and what its customers feel, turning a category-wide liability into something that looks a great deal like advantage. The newer entrants competing to shave the trial-scale curve into a slightly longer number may be winning, at considerable expense, an argument their patients were never having, while the more valuable ground, the honest and specific conversation about movement, natural expression, and how a good result should behave across its life, sits there unclaimed.

The Marga Perspective

There is a general principle underneath the specific case, and it is one of the more useful things I know about this industry: the endpoint that governs a product's commercial fate is the one the patient uses, not the one the regulator accepts, and any company that lets the regulatory endpoint stand in for the market endpoint will keep being punished for performance it genuinely delivered. Aesthetics is unusual among medical categories precisely because the person being treated is also the person grading the outcome, in real time, by feel and by sight, against a standard she never wrote down and cannot fully articulate yet applies without mercy. A firm that wants to win here has to do the harder work of discovering what that standard actually is for a given treatment, and then aligning everything to it, its measurement, its claims, its training, and its patient education, so that the company and the patient are finally watching the same clock. The alternative is to keep building better answers to a question the market has stopped asking.

Questions worth sitting with

Anyone responsible for a neurotoxin, or for any aesthetic product judged more by feel than by figures, would do well to sit with a few questions before the next round of duration claims is written.

  1. What is the endpoint your patients are genuinely using to decide whether this product still works, and how far does it sit from the endpoint on your label?
  2. Are your duration claims true in the study and true in the mirror, or only the first of those?
  3. Where in the life of a good result does the patient's experience diverge from your data, and what would it take to measure that divergence rather than ignore it?
  4. And the largest question, the one that reframes the others: are you optimizing the number that earns the approval or the number that earns the rebooking, because in this category those are not the same number, and only one of them compounds.
Beyond the article

The wrong-endpoint problem is one example of a habit that runs through the whole of aesthetics, the habit of measuring what is convenient and then mistaking it for what matters, and untangling that, working out which endpoint a given product is really being judged on and rebuilding the strategy around it, is a great deal of what I do with companies whose clinical performance and commercial performance have quietly stopped agreeing with each other. If you have a product that studies well and sells stubbornly, or that patients abandon for reasons your data cannot explain, the answer is usually not more data but a better ruler, and that is a conversation worth having.
