Every Great Product Starts Dying the Day It’s Approved

Approval is not a finish line. It is the moment the clock starts.

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

The day a product is approved, everyone celebrates. They should be a little nervous. Approval is the instant a product stops being a possibility and becomes a perishable asset: one with competitors, a patent clock, and a market that will forget it faster than anyone expects.

Most teams treat launch as the summit. In reality it is the trailhead of a managed decline, one that good strategy slows and bad strategy accelerates. The honest question on approval day is not how to celebrate. It is how to compound.

A product’s value is not set at approval. It is set by how deliberately you manage the decline that begins there.

The decline starts on day one

Every advantage a product holds at launch erodes on a schedule you can predict. Novelty fades as the category adjusts to it. Exclusivity ends on a date that is already on the calendar. Share of voice decays the moment a competitor decides to outspend you. None of this is bad luck. It is closer to physics, and physics can be planned for.

The teams that endure treat launch day as the start of a countdown they have already prepared for. They know which data will still matter in year five, which indication opens the next market, and which line extension resets the clock. The plan for the second act exists before the first act ships, because by the time decline is visible in the numbers, the cheap moves are gone.

Managing decline is a discipline, not a mood

Managing decline sounds pessimistic. It is the opposite. It is the confidence to invest in a product’s future while it is still winning, instead of scrambling once growth stalls. The best lifecycle work happens when a product looks healthiest, because that is precisely when you still have the budget, the attention, and the runway to shape what comes next.

Waiting until the curve bends is how good products die quietly. The organization notices the slowdown, cuts support to protect margin, and accelerates the very decline it was worried about. Momentum, once lost in this category, is extraordinarily expensive to rebuild.

The worst time to start planning a product’s second act is the moment its first act stops working.

Lifecycle strategy, then, is not a late-stage chore handed to whoever is free. It is the discipline of deciding, early and on purpose, how a product will stay relevant long after the launch noise has faded. The clock starts on approval day whether you manage it or not. The only choice is whether you are the one holding it.
