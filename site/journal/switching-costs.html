How Companies Build Switching Costs

Retention is not earned by satisfaction. It is engineered.

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners

Companies love to explain retention with the word satisfaction. Our customers stay because they are happy. Satisfaction is real, and it is also dangerously fragile, because a happier competitor is always one launch away. The companies that keep customers over the long run do not rely on happiness alone. They make leaving quietly costly, and they design those costs deliberately.

This sounds cynical until you look at how the best switching costs actually feel from the customer’s side. Done well, they are indistinguishable from benefits. Accumulated loyalty value, a technique the provider has mastered, a workflow the product has been woven into, data that now lives inside a system, a brand the patient requests by name: each of these gives the customer something real, and each one, at the same time, quietly raises the price of walking away.

Loyalty is not the absence of a reason to leave. It is the presence of a cost to leaving.

The forms a switching cost takes

The forms are worth naming because most companies build only one and leave the others on the table. There are economic switching costs, like loyalty balances and volume-based pricing. There are learned switching costs, the accumulated skill and comfort a provider has with your product that a competitor would force them to rebuild. There are integration switching costs, where your product is embedded in a practice’s systems and daily workflow. And there are reputational ones, where the patient specifically asks for your brand, so switching means disappointing the customer’s own expectation. The strongest franchises stack several of these at once.

What makes stacking so powerful is that the costs defend one another. A competitor might beat your price, neutralizing the economic cost, only to run into the learned cost, the provider does not want to relearn technique, and behind that the reputational cost, the patients are asking for your name. Each layer buys time, and time lets the others deepen. A single switching cost is a hurdle. Several, reinforcing, are a wall.

Design the cost of leaving

The art, and the ethics, of this is to build switching costs customers experience as value rather than as traps. A loyalty program that genuinely rewards, a tool that genuinely fits the day, a result the provider genuinely trusts: these keep people not because they are trapped but because leaving means giving up something good and starting over. That distinction matters. Switching costs built on real value are durable. Switching costs built on lock-in without value breed resentment and eventually revolt.

Every durable franchise, examined closely, turns out to be a carefully constructed set of reasons to stay, most of which the customer would describe as things they like. That is not an accident. It is the quiet, deliberate engineering that separates companies that have to re-win their customers every year from companies whose customers cannot easily imagine leaving.

The best switching costs do not feel like a trap. They feel like something you would lose, which is exactly why they work.

Retention, in the end, is not something you earn once and keep. It is something you build, layer by layer, into the product, the program, the workflow, and the brand, until leaving means starting over. Satisfaction gets a customer to stay this year. Engineered switching costs, built on genuine value, get them to stay for the next ten.
