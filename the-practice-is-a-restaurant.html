The Best-Run Aesthetic Practice Is a Restaurant

Medicine Is the License to Operate; Hospitality Is the Actual Business

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners
Practice operationsHospitalityPatient experienceRetentionUnit economics

Walk into the most successful aesthetic practices in the country and pay attention to what is actually producing their success, and you will notice that the thing setting them apart is only partly clinical. The injecting is excellent, certainly, but so is the injecting at plenty of struggling practices, and what distinguishes the ones that thrive is something the medical framing of the business entirely fails to capture: the way they are run resembles a great restaurant far more than it resembles a clinic. The greeting, the atmosphere, the rhythm of the visit, the consistency of the experience from one appointment to the next, the sense that you are a valued regular rather than a case, the seamless choreography of front of house and back of house, all of it belongs to the language of hospitality rather than medicine, and the practices that understand this, whether they articulate it or not, are operating on a model that their more clinically minded competitors do not even recognize they are competing against. The medical license is what allows the doors to open; hospitality is what actually happens once they do, and it is the business.

The kitchen is necessary and not sufficient

None of this diminishes the clinical work, and the analogy would be worthless if it did, because a restaurant with beautiful service and inedible food fails exactly as reliably as one with wonderful food and hostile service. Clinical excellence is the kitchen, the non-negotiable foundation without which nothing else matters, and a practice that produces poor outcomes cannot hospitality its way to success any more than a restaurant can plate its way out of bad cooking. The point of the analogy is not that the clinical work is unimportant but that it is necessary and not sufficient, the price of entry rather than the source of advantage, because once a certain threshold of clinical competence is met, and it is met by a great many practices, the competition is decided almost entirely on the dimensions that restaurants have understood for a century and that aesthetic practices, hiding behind their white coats, have barely begun to take seriously. The best food in town does not guarantee the best restaurant, and the best injector in town does not guarantee the best practice, because in both cases the product being sold is an experience, and the food is only part of it.

Consistency is the entire game

Ask what actually separates a great restaurant from a merely competent one and the answer is consistency, the ability to deliver the same excellent dish on a Tuesday in February as on a Saturday in June, to make the experience reliable enough that a guest can trust it before they walk in, and this is precisely the quality that separates a great aesthetic practice from a competent one as well. A practice that delivers a beautiful result sometimes, depending on which injector, which day, which mood, has the same problem as a restaurant whose kitchen is brilliant when the head chef is in and mediocre when they are not, which is that it cannot be trusted, and trust is the thing a repeat business runs on. This is where the restaurant framing connects to everything else that governs this category, because consistency is how trust is built, and the practices that win are the ones that have engineered their operations so that the experience does not depend on the heroics of any single person on any given day, the same way a great restaurant group builds systems so that the food and the service survive the absence of the founding chef. The failure to build that consistency is exactly why so many attempts to scale aesthetic practices falter, industrializing the volume while losing the reliability, growing the number of covers while degrading the meal, which is the same mistake a restaurant group makes when it expands faster than it can protect the thing that made the original work.

The economics are hospitality economics

Look at how a great practice actually makes money and the resemblance deepens, because the economics are throughput-and-repeat economics, the economics of a restaurant, not the fee-for-service economics of a clinic. A restaurant lives on covers per night, table turns, average check, and the lifetime value of a regular who comes back every month, and a practice lives on the identical structure wearing different words, patients per day, chair utilization, average ticket, and the lifetime value of a member who returns on a reliable cadence. Both businesses are throughput businesses layered on top of repeat businesses, where the profit comes not from any single transaction but from the volume of transactions and the reliability with which customers return, and both are therefore governed by the operational disciplines that throughput-and-repeat businesses require, the scheduling, the flow, the capacity management, the membership and loyalty structures that convert a one-time guest into a regular. A practice that understands itself as a clinic will price and schedule like a clinic, episodically, transactionally, and leave most of its economics on the table; a practice that understands itself as a restaurant will build the recurring, throughput-optimized, loyalty-driven engine that the model actually rewards.

The chef does not scale, and neither does the injector

The final piece of the analogy is the one the industry most needs to hear, because it resolves a problem the category has been circling for years, the problem of the celebrity injector. A restaurant built entirely around a celebrity chef has a ceiling and a fragility, because the chef cannot be in two places at once, cannot work forever, and takes the entire value of the enterprise with them when they leave, which is why the most durable restaurant businesses are the ones that figured out how to build systems, training, and consistency robust enough that the experience survives the chef, turning individual genius into a repeatable operation. Aesthetics is living through the same lesson in its own vocabulary, discovering that the practice built around a single star injector has the same ceiling and the same fragility, and that the durable model is the one that builds the trusted system rather than depending on the trusted individual, the same conclusion that the fragility of authority in this category keeps pointing toward from every direction. The great restaurant group and the great aesthetic group solve the identical problem in the identical way, by making the experience a property of the system rather than the person, and the practices that learn this will scale in a way that the celebrity-injector model never can.

The Marga Perspective

The aesthetics industry has miscategorized itself, filing its best practices under medicine when they are far more accurately understood as hospitality businesses that happen to require a medical license, and this miscategorization quietly causes practices to underinvest in exactly the things that determine their success, the experience, the consistency, the throughput economics, the systems that let excellence survive any individual. A practice that recognizes itself as a restaurant will build the front of house as deliberately as the back, engineer consistency so that trust can form, structure its economics around throughput and repeat patronage, and construct the systems that let it scale without losing the thing that made it worth scaling, while its competitors keep treating the business as a series of clinical episodes and wondering why their clinical excellence does not translate into commercial success. Clinical skill is the kitchen and it must be excellent, but the business has always been hospitality, and the practices that understand which one they are actually running will win against the ones that never realized there was a question.

Questions worth sitting with

Any practice owner or operator serious about the business should sit with a few questions the medical framing tends to obscure.

  1. If your clinical outcomes are strong but your growth is not, how much of the gap lives in the experience, the consistency, and the hospitality that your competitors may be doing better?
  2. Can a patient trust your result before they walk in, or does it depend on which injector and which day, and what would it take to make the experience a property of your system rather than your stars?
  3. Are you running throughput-and-repeat economics deliberately, with the scheduling, capacity, and loyalty structures that model rewards, or are you leaving them on the table by pricing like a clinic?
  4. And the question that reframes the whole enterprise: are you running a clinic that happens to be pleasant, or a restaurant that happens to require a medical license, because only one of those is built to win?
Beyond the article

The recognition that a great aesthetic practice is a hospitality business rather than a medical one reshapes how it should be built, staffed, priced, and scaled, and helping practices and the companies that serve them build the consistency, the experience, and the systems that actually drive success, rather than relying on clinical excellence alone to carry a business that is really about hospitality, is a meaningful part of what I do. If your outcomes are strong but your practice is not growing the way they should let it, the gap is often in the parts of the business that medicine never taught you to see, and that is a good place to begin a conversation.
