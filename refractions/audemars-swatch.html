What Aesthetics Can Learn From a $400 Audemars Piguet

The Gateway Product That Recruits Buyers Instead of Cannibalizing Them

A
Aneesh Patel, PharmDFounder, Marga Partners
Gateway productBrand extensionRecruitmentAccessibilityLuxury

Refractions: what aesthetics can learn from industries that have nothing to do with it.

Audemars Piguet makes watches that cost as much as houses, complicated horological objects with waiting lists and an identity built, above everything else, on scarcity and exclusivity, and in 2026 it did something that by the ordinary logic of luxury should have been unthinkable, partnering with Swatch, a brand whose watches can cost less than a hundred dollars, to release a collection of playful pocket watches modeled on its iconic Royal Oak for roughly four hundred dollars. The result was mayhem in the best sense, lines forming outside stores around the world, immediate sellouts, and a resale market that priced the four-hundred-dollar objects at many times their cost, and the reaction from luxury purists was the predictable anxiety that a heritage house had cheapened itself, diluted its exclusivity, and handed its sacred design to the masses. The purists had it backward, because the accessible collaboration did not diminish demand for the expensive originals; it did the opposite, and the mechanism by which an inexpensive gateway product strengthens rather than weakens a premium brand is a lesson aesthetics badly needs, because the industry fears the accessible entry point as dilution when it is very often recruitment.

The gateway builds the aspiration it seems to cheapen

The intuition that an accessible version of a luxury product must cannibalize the expensive one rests on a model of demand that is simply wrong for aspirational categories, because it assumes a fixed pool of buyers choosing between the cheap version and the expensive one, when what actually happens is that the accessible product introduces the brand to an enormous audience that would never have encountered it otherwise and plants an aspiration that the expensive version later harvests. The precedent was already clear from an earlier collaboration in the same watch world, after which sales of the premium original rose rather than fell, and the reasoning is straightforward once stated: a person who buys the four-hundred-dollar homage is not someone who would otherwise have bought the fifty-thousand-dollar original and is now satisfied with less; that person is someone who has just been recruited into the brand's universe, taught to desire it, and set on a path that, for some meaningful fraction, ends years later at the real thing. The gateway does not sate the aspiration; it creates it, converting a mass audience that was previously outside the brand entirely into a population of future premium buyers, which is why the accessible product grows the expensive one rather than competing with it.

The core survives because provenance is intact

The reason the luxury house can do this without actually cheapening itself is that the accessible collaboration is carefully bounded and the exclusivity of the core remains untouched, so the four-hundred-dollar object is unmistakably a playful homage rather than a substitute, and the fifty-thousand-dollar original loses none of its scarcity, its craftsmanship, or its status. This is the same principle that explains why belief and provenance, rather than measurable specification, are what sustain a premium, because the value of the expensive original was never in features that the cheap version could replicate; it was in the exclusivity and the provenance that the collaboration deliberately leaves intact. The gateway product borrows the brand's aura to recruit new admirers while the core retains everything that made the aura worth having, and the two coexist precisely because the accessible version never pretends to be the real thing and the real thing never surrenders what makes it real, which is why the collaboration expands the audience without diluting the exclusivity that the audience is being taught to desire.

Aesthetics fears its own gateway

Aesthetics should recognize its own anxiety in the luxury purists' misplaced fear, because the industry frequently treats accessible entry points, the lower-commitment treatment, the introductory offering, the gateway procedure, as threats that will cheapen the category or cannibalize its premium work, when they are very often the single most effective mechanism for recruiting new patients into a category most people have never entered. An accessible, lower-barrier aesthetic offering is not a discount that devalues the premium work; it is a gateway that introduces the enormous unpenetrated majority to the category, plants an aspiration, and sets some meaningful fraction on a path toward more involved and more premium treatment over time, exactly as the four-hundred-dollar watch sets its buyers on a path toward the fifty-thousand-dollar one. This connects directly to the recognition that the category's greatest opportunity is recruiting the vast population that has never participated, and to the understanding that an accessible hook can pull people into a premium world rather than degrading it, and the industry's instinct to protect its exclusivity by refusing the gateway is precisely the instinct that keeps the category small, mistaking recruitment for dilution and forgoing the mechanism that actually builds the premium demand it is trying to protect.

The gateway and the core are a system

The deepest lesson the collaboration teaches is that the accessible product and the premium core are not competitors but a system, a funnel in which the gateway recruits and the core harvests, and a brand that understands this designs the two to work together rather than treating the accessible offering as a threat to the exclusive one. Aesthetics could build the same system deliberately, an accessible entry point engineered to recruit new patients into the category paired with a premium tier that the aspiration those patients develop eventually leads them toward, the gateway and the core reinforcing each other exactly as the playful watch and the serious one do. The industry's failure to build this system, its tendency to guard the premium by refusing the accessible, is a failure to understand that in an aspirational category the cheap version is not the enemy of the expensive one but its most effective recruiter, and that the brands which grasp this, in watches and in aesthetics alike, grow the exclusive core precisely by opening an accessible door to it.

Beyond the article

The fear that an accessible entry point will cheapen a premium category, when it is very often the most effective way to recruit the future premium customer, is one of the most costly misunderstandings in aesthetics, and helping companies and practices design the gateway-and-core system that recruits new patients without diluting the premium work, turning accessibility into aspiration rather than discount, is a meaningful part of what I do. If you have been protecting your premium positioning by refusing an accessible entry point, that instinct may be quietly costing you the very demand you are trying to build, and it is a good place to begin a conversation.
